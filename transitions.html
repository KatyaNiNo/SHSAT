Transition Words: The Partial Agreement Trap
Reasoning Skill Builder — SAT Reading & Writing
The Core Concept

Why this type of question trips students up

Some transition questions set a trap using a two-part prediction in the first sentence. The second sentence then confirms one part of the prediction while quietly contradicting the other. Students who catch only the confirmed part walk straight into the trap and choose a causation or addition word. The correct answer is almost always a contrast word, because the full picture is not what was expected.

Map the Prediction rule: Before choosing any transition, list every claim sentence 1 makes. Then check sentence 2 against each one. If even one claim is contradicted, the relationship is contrast, not confirmation.

Worked Example

Let's map the original question

Sentence 1 According to climate envelope models, individual organisms that inhabit the coolest areas of their species' geographic distribution are expected to benefit from a warming climate, while individuals in the warmest areas will struggle.

Sentence 2 A 2024 University of Arizona study,        found that warmer-than-average temperatures resulted in a decrease in growth across all areas of the common piñon tree's distribution range.

Prediction map

Prediction (sentence 1)
Prediction A
Cool-area organisms will benefit from warming
Prediction B
Warm-area organisms will struggle from warming
→
What the study actually found
Contradicted
All areas showed decreased growth. Cool areas did NOT benefit.
Confirmed
Growth also declined in warm areas, consistent with "will struggle."
✗ One prediction confirmed, one contradicted. The overall relationship is contrast. Correct answer: A (though)

Students who pick B (therefore) or D (for example) are only seeing Prediction B confirmed. They miss that cool-area organisms were supposed to benefit but did not. "Though" signals that the study's finding cuts against what the full model would lead you to expect.

Drill 1

Apply the Map the Prediction rule

Nutrition researchers have long maintained that reducing saturated fat intake lowers the risk of cardiovascular disease, while a diet consistently high in saturated fat accelerates arterial damage over time. A large-scale review of 27 clinical trials published last year,        found no meaningful difference in cardiovascular outcomes between participants who significantly reduced saturated fat and those who maintained their usual diet.
Correct. Map the two predictions: (1) reducing saturated fat lowers cardiovascular risk, and (2) high saturated fat accelerates damage. The review contradicts prediction 1 by finding no improvement from reducing saturated fat. That contradiction makes this a contrast relationship, so "though" is right. Students who pick A or D are anchoring only on the "high saturated fat is bad" part and ignoring whether the low-fat intervention actually helped.
Not quite. Map both predictions first. Sentence 1 claims that (1) reducing saturated fat lowers risk and (2) high saturated fat causes damage. The review found no benefit from reducing saturated fat, so prediction 1 was contradicted, even if prediction 2 still holds. When any claim from sentence 1 is overturned, the transition should signal contrast. The answer is B (though).
Drill 2

One more rep

Marine biologists predicted that rising ocean temperatures would reduce fish populations in tropical waters while simultaneously allowing species to expand their ranges into previously too-cold polar regions. A decade-long survey of Pacific fish populations,        documented a decline in species diversity across both tropical and polar zones alike.
Correct. Map both predictions: (1) tropical populations would decline, and (2) polar populations would expand. The survey confirmed the tropical decline but contradicted the polar expansion, since polar zones declined too. That makes this a contrast relationship. This is the same trap as the piñon tree question: one prediction confirmed, one contradicted, so the transition must be a contrast word. The answer is C (however).
Not quite. Map out both predictions. The biologists said (1) tropical fish populations would fall and (2) polar populations would expand. The survey found declines in both zones. Prediction 1 was confirmed, but prediction 2 was directly contradicted. Because at least one claim is overturned, the relationship is contrast. The answer is C (however).
