Segment 1
Intro & Warm-Up
20 min

The tutor introduces themselves briefly, then opens it to the class. Go around the room and ask each student: their name, their school, their strongest section, and their weakest section (math or verbal). Keep it moving and keep the tone light. The goal is to get everyone talking and to flag where each student thinks they stand.

Then pivot to the big question: "Do you think the SHSAT is going to be like any test you have studied for before?" Let them answer. Then explain.

The SHSAT is a Digital Question Adaptive Test. The test is watching you while you take it. Answer the first 10 ELA questions correctly and the algorithm locks you into a high range for that section. Same for math. Those first 10 questions on each section are the most important questions on the entire exam — and we do not know the topics or the difficulty in advance.

✎ Write on the board: First 10 ELA + First 10 Math = Your range.

Every test they have taken so far in middle school has been topic-focused: one chapter, one unit, one skill. The SHSAT is cumulative. Any math concept from 4th grade through 8th grade could show up on question one. That includes:

Arithmetic Absolute value Inequalities Negative numbers Word problems Ratios Geometry

There is no way to know what comes first, which is exactly why preparation has to look different here. The best thing students can do for math is keep every skill agile through flashcards and constant repetition. For verbal, the system is 5BLX — and that is what this class is built around.

Two more things that make the SHSAT unlike any test they have taken before. First, students cannot go back once they leave a reading passage. On most tests, you can jump around freely. Not here. Once you move past a passage, those questions are locked. Second, reading passages on the SHSAT are not uniform — a passage can carry 4, 5, or 7 questions. Students need to know this going in so they are not caught off guard.

Segment 4
Exit Ticket
10 min

Close the class by going around the room. Each student says out loud two things: one thing they learned today and one thing they are taking away from this class. No hands raised, no volunteering — everyone answers.

The teacher tosses the ball or crumpled newspaper to the first student. That student answers, then tosses it to the next person. Keep it moving until everyone has gone.

This is how every session ends. It keeps the energy up and makes sure every student walks out with something concrete.
